  ONE-PAGE PROJECT SUMMARY  (plain English)

TITLE
  Intelligent Traffic Light System Using Deep Reinforcement Learning -
  A Lagos (Ikeja) Case Study.

PROBLEM
  Conventional traffic lights run on fixed timers and cannot react to changing
  traffic. In a city like Lagos, where demand on each road varies wildly, this
  wastes green time and worsens congestion and delay.

AIM
  Build and evaluate a traffic-light controller that learns to adapt its signal
  decisions to real-time demand, and show it outperforms the standard fixed-time
  controller.

APPROACH (METHODOLOGY)
  - Simulator: SUMO (open-source urban traffic simulator).
  - Test site: a real intersection in Ikeja, Lagos, imported from OpenStreetMap.
  - Controller: a Deep Q-Network (DQN) reinforcement-learning agent.
  - Signal: realistic 4-phase protected signal - each approach gets its own
    green (action_dim = 4), as the real junction operates.
  - State (input): 14 values - queue, waiting time and density per approach,
    plus the current phase and how long it has been active (phase-aware).
  - Action (output): which of the 4 protected green phases to run next.
  - Reward: reduction in congestion (queue + waiting time), minus a small
    penalty for switching phases too often.
  - Training: two stages - (1) imitation pre-training to copy a sensible expert
    rule for a fast start, then (2) reinforcement-learning fine-tuning for 200
    episodes so the agent learns to plan ahead.
  - Baseline for comparison: a standard fixed-time signal on identical traffic.

KEY RESULT
  On the REAL Ikeja junction (OpenStreetMap geometry) with the REALISTIC 4-phase
  protected signal and variable demand, the trained phase-aware DQN reduced
  average queue by ~14% (7.62 vs 8.81) and average waiting time by ~16% (143.8
  vs 171.4) compared to the fixed-time baseline, in simulation - winning on both.
  The edge comes from real-time adaptation: skipping empty approaches instead of
  serving them on a fixed cycle.

PERCEPTION
  A YOLOv8 vehicle detector turns a camera feed into the per-approach vehicle
  counts the controller needs. This was demonstrated live - detecting and
  counting real vehicles from a camera/video - and is light enough (YOLOv8-nano)
  to run on a Raspberry Pi.

GENERALISATION
  The approach was tested across several demand patterns on the faithful 4-phase
  junction. It cut queue and waiting time together when demand was uneven and
  variable - evidence of a learned strategy, not a memorised case. (Against a
  perfectly-tuned Webster fixed timer it ties on average queue but still wins on
  waiting time through adaptation; on over-saturated demand no signal policy can
  win, as the junction is capacity-bound.)

LIMITATIONS
  - Single junction, single demand profile -> does not transfer zero-shot to
    other geometries; weights are intersection-specific.
  - Only wins when traffic is below saturation and asymmetric; at gridlock the
    junction is capacity-bound and no signal policy can help.
  - Hardware deployment (camera -> Pi -> physical lights) is designed but the
    GPIO/logging bridges are not yet implemented.

FUTURE WORK
  - Train across many junctions and demand patterns (domain randomisation).
  - Deploy on a Raspberry Pi with a camera (YOLO detection) driving real lights
    via GPIO, with a database log and a safe fixed-time fallback.
  - Add emergency-vehicle priority and pedestrian phases.

CONTRIBUTION
  A working, evaluated demonstration that a learning-based adaptive signal
  controller substantially outperforms fixed-time control under the uneven
  traffic conditions typical of Lagos - with an honest account of where it
  helps, where it doesn't, and what it would take to deploy.

TECH STACK
  Python, PyTorch (DQN), SUMO + TraCI (simulation), YOLOv8 (vehicle detection),
  Matplotlib (figures/animation). Raspberry Pi + GPIO targeted for deployment.
