--- THE BASICS -----------------------------------------------------------------

Q: In one sentence, what did you do?
A: I trained a Deep Q-Network to control the lights at a real Ikeja, Lagos
   junction - a realistic 4-phase signal - in SUMO, and it cut average queue
   ~14% and average wait ~16% versus a fixed-time timer, while adapting to
   changing traffic.

Q: Why is this problem worth solving?
A: Lagos traffic is among the worst in the world. Most signals run on fixed
   timers that ignore real demand, so they waste green time on empty approaches
   while the busy approach jams. An adaptive controller fixes exactly that.

Q: What is reinforcement learning, simply?
A: Learning by trial and error. The agent tries an action, gets a reward
   (less congestion = good), and over many attempts learns a policy that
   maximises long-term reward.

Q: What is a Deep Q-Network (DQN)?
A: A neural network that estimates the value (expected future reward) of each
   possible action given the current traffic state. The agent picks the action
   with the highest predicted value.

--- THE DESIGN -----------------------------------------------------------------

Q: What exactly is the state, action and reward?
A: STATE = 12 numbers: queue length, waiting time and density for each of the
   4 approaches. ACTION = which green phase to run next. REWARD = drop in queue
   and waiting time, with a small penalty for switching phases too frequently.

Q: Why those 12 features?
A: They capture how congested each direction is and how long people have waited
   - the two things a good signal must balance. They're also obtainable from a
   camera, so the design transfers to real hardware.

Q: Why a penalty for switching phases?
A: Without it the agent could flicker the lights rapidly, which is unsafe and
   unrealistic. The penalty makes it commit to a phase long enough to be useful.

Q: How does it decide? Continuously?
A: It makes a decision every few simulation seconds (the decision interval),
   not every single frame - matching how real adaptive signals work.

--- TRAINING -------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: Walk me through how you trained it.
A: Two stages. First, imitation pre-training: the network copies a sensible
   expert rule until it matches it 100% - this gives a fast, safe starting
   point. Second, RL fine-tuning for 200 episodes with gamma 0.99, which lets
   it plan ahead and beat the greedy expert.

Q: Why not just RL from scratch?
A: Pure RL from random weights is slow and unstable on this problem. Warm-
   starting from imitation made training faster and more reliable - a standard
   technique (imitation + RL fine-tuning).

Q: What did the training curves show?
A: Reward rising and queue/wait falling over episodes until they plateaued -
   see media\training_curves_peak.png. The plateau is why I stopped at 200.

--- RESULTS & VALIDITY ---------------------------------------------------------

Q: Are these numbers realistic?
A: Yes - the headline -14% queue / -16% wait is on the REAL Ikeja geometry with
   the REALISTIC 4-phase protected signal and variable demand. That's
   deliberately the honest case, not an idealised one. RL traffic studies report
   a wide range; modest, consistent gains on a faithful high-capacity junction
   are exactly what the literature would predict.

Q: Where does the improvement come from?
A: RESPONSIVENESS. The model adapts in real time and skips empty approaches
   instead of serving them on a fixed cycle, so it wins on BOTH queue (~14%) and
   waiting time (~16%). Against a perfectly-tuned (Webster) fixed timer it
   roughly ties on average queue but still wins on waiting time - real-time
   adaptation to fluctuating arrivals is the genuine edge a fixed plan can't match.

Q: How do you know it isn't just memorising?
A: I tested the approach across several demand patterns on the real geometry and
   the pattern held: it cuts queue ~20-31% and wins on waiting time wherever
   demand is asymmetric, and correctly adds little when traffic is balanced -
   evidence of a learned strategy, not a memorised case.

Q: What is the biggest limitation?
A: It's trained on one junction with one demand profile, so it doesn't transfer
   zero-shot to other geometries (weights are intersection-specific) and it only
   wins when traffic is below saturation and asymmetric. At gridlock no signal
   policy can help - it's capacity-bound. Fix: train across many junctions and
   demand patterns (domain randomisation).

Q: What's your baseline and is it fair?
A: A fixed-time controller cycling on a timer - the standard in Lagos today -
   run on the exact same traffic as the AI. Same junction, same cars, only the
   controller differs.

--- REAL WORLD / HARDWARE ------------------------------------------------------

Q: Could this run in real life?
A: Yes, that's the intended path: a camera + YOLO detector turns the live scene
   into the same 12-number state, the trained DQN picks the phase, and a
   Raspberry Pi drives the physical lights through GPIO, logging decisions to a
   database. The detection and control code already exist; the GPIO/logging
   bridges are designed as callbacks and are the next implementation step.

Q: How would the controller know the traffic in real life? (the camera/YOLO)
A: A camera on each approach runs a YOLOv8 vehicle detector that counts cars,
   buses, motorcycles and trucks; those counts become the same state vector the
   DQN reads in simulation. I can demo the detection live - it boxes and counts
   real vehicles from a camera or video feed (see the YOLO demo). YOLOv8-nano is
   light enough to run on a Raspberry Pi, the deployment target.

Q: Why SUMO instead of testing on a real junction?
A: Safety and cost. You cannot let an untrained agent control a live junction.
   SUMO lets you train over thousands of episodes and millions of vehicles
   safely, then transfer the trained policy.

Q: What about emergencies / pedestrians / sensor failure?
A: Out of scope for this prototype, and honest about it. The design can extend:
   an emergency-vehicle override, pedestrian phases, and a safe fixed-time
   fallback if the camera fails. (An emergency-detector experiment exists in
   the codebase.)
